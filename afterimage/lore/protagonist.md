# AFTERIMAGE — Protagonist Architecture

---

## WHAT SHE IS (Functionally)

She is a **projection surface**, not a character.

Kershaw's research principle: the less you show of a figure, the more the viewer inserts themselves.
The protagonist must be shown at the edge of perception — enough to feel present, not enough to feel specific.

This is not stylistic modesty. It is mechanical. The moment she has a face, a name, a clear personality — the audience watches her. Without those, the audience **becomes** her.

---

## VISUAL RULES (Never Break)

- **Face:** Never shown clearly. Profile only. Always partially obscured — hair, shadow, foreground object, motion blur at the moment of turn. The eye flickers toward the camera and away.
- **Body:** Shown from behind most frequently. Walking. Standing at windows. Sitting with back to frame.
- **Hands:** The exception — hands can be shown clearly. Close-up of hands holding things (the cassette, a phone screen, a coffee cup, a note). Hands are the only specific part of her.
- **Voice:** Never. She does not speak in any video. The closest she comes is the sound of breathing in a quiet space, or footsteps stopping.
- **Clothing:** Consistent palette per phase. Dark and muted, nothing identifying. Worn-in, not fashion.
- **Scale:** She is almost always small in frame against the city. The city is large. She is navigating it.

---

## EMOTIONAL SIGNATURE

She does not perform emotion. The spaces perform emotion around her.

When she is in grief: the space is wet, flat light, longer takes.
When she is searching: the city feels more active around her, she moves faster.
When she finds something: stillness. She stops. The video ends 2 seconds after she stops.

**The Kershaw principle applied:** we never see the emotion land. We see the body right before it lands. The audience completes the feeling from their own experience.

---

## THE PROJECTION MECHANISM

Every viewer decision creates a slightly different protagonist:
- The viewer who lost someone projects that loss onto the Other One
- The viewer who left a city projects their city onto the unnamed one  
- The viewer who found an old letter projects their own letter onto the handwriting
- The viewer who is lonely at night recognizes the 2am convenience store

She holds all of these simultaneously because she holds none of them specifically.

**This is why the no-face rule is absolute.** A face closes the space. Without a face, she stays open.

---

## WHAT SHE CARRIES (The Three Objects)

These appear regularly in her hands. They are the tangible evidence of her interiority.

**1. The Cassette Tape**
An old tape with no label. She holds it. Sometimes plays it (we never hear what's on it clearly — just the texture of recorded sound through worn speakers). Sometimes just looks at it.

**2. The Phone**
Shows a screen, never legible. Not a ringing phone, not a texting phone. A phone with a photo on it she keeps looking at. Or a phone with a message she keeps not sending.

**3. The Note**
Paper. Folded. In the same handwriting that appears across the universe. She reads it. We cannot read it. She folds it back. She puts it away.

---

## WHAT SHE DOESN'T DO

- She doesn't look at the camera except by near-accident
- She doesn't react to the viewer — she is not performing
- She doesn't resolve her emotions on screen
- She doesn't explain, even non-verbally
- She doesn't speak to The Other One directly in any video (even if they might be present)

---

## THE IDENTITY PARADOX

She is the most specific person in the universe (consistent objects, consistent spaces, consistent palette) and the least defined person in the universe (no face, no name, no explicit backstory).

This is Entergalactic's technique inverted: Entergalactic painted the city with precise color to make it feel inhabited. AFTERIMAGE makes the protagonist precise in texture but empty in identity — so she feels inhabited by the viewer.
