# AFTERIMAGE — Visual DNA
## Found by studying what repeats across all six references.
## This is not a prompt document. This is a seeing document.

---

## THE METHOD

Do not look at what these creators make.
Look at what their frames have in common when stripped of subject matter.

Ignore: story, character, lore, platform, genre.
Look only at: light, shadow, color, grain, composition, the quality of the image itself.

---

## THE SIX — WHAT THEIR FRAMES ACTUALLY DO

---

### KERSHAW

The frame feels warm in one specific place and cold everywhere else.

The warmth is always a body — hands, the side of a face, the curve of a shoulder. The body is the only source of warmth in the frame. Everything behind it, beside it, above it is cooler. Not dramatically cool. Slightly. The differential is what creates the feeling.

His grain is real. It does not sit on top of the image. It is inside the image. The grain has weight — it is heavier in the shadows, lighter in the highlights, and it moves differently in both. This is how you know it was captured, not processed.

His lights are never sources you question. A window. A streetlight. A screen. Something that exists in the world and happens to be in the frame. The light was not placed there for the shot. It was found.

His compositions are slightly wrong. Not badly framed — slightly off from where a trained photographer would put the subject. The subject is too far left. Or there is too much sky. Or the focus is on the wrong thing. This is not accident. It is the feeling of someone who looked at a moment and captured it before they could think about where to put it.

**What repeats in Kershaw's frames:**
The single warm thing in a cold field. The grain that lives inside the image. The light that wasn't placed. The composition that arrived before it was designed.

---

### ENTERGALACTIC

The city is not depicted. The city is felt.

Every exterior frame has at least three planes of depth: a dark foreground (a building silhouette, a fire escape, a water tower — nearly black), a lit middle distance (the buildings where people are, the windows glowing amber and orange), and a far atmospheric layer (the glow of the entire city against a deep purple-indigo sky). These three planes create the feeling that the city goes on further than the frame.

The shadow color is not black. It is deep purple — approximately #1a0d2e. This is the most important fact about Entergalactic's visual language. When you darken toward black in this world, you travel through blue and purple, not through gray. The darkness is chromatic. It has a direction.

The warmth is always in the windows. The specific amber-orange of inhabited spaces — light from inside, spilling out — against the purple-blue of the exterior. This is the thermal map of a city: inhabited spaces warm, uninhabited spaces cold.

Their frames are painted. Not cartoon-painted — the surfaces have texture. A slight roughness, as if the image was made by hand and has the evidence of hands in it.

**What repeats in Entergalactic's frames:**
Three planes of depth. Purple-black shadow (not gray-black). Warm inhabited windows against cold uninhabited city. The texture of a made thing.

---

### ANUKITSU

Light does not stay where it is placed.

In anukitsu's frames, light sources bleed. A candle does not illuminate a circle around itself — it sends warmth into the surrounding air. The air near the light source is slightly tinted, slightly alive. The further you move from the source, the more the world cools and softens. But the transition is not sharp. It is a gradient that continues.

The grain in these frames is soft. Not film grain — more like the texture of paper. If you put your finger on the image, the grain would feel like texture, not like noise. This creates: the image feels like it was made from a physical material, not captured by a sensor.

Subjects are never fully resolved. There is always something slightly soft — not out of focus, but as if the image remembers that it is made of light and light is not precise. The edges of things bleed slightly into what surrounds them.

**What repeats in anukitsu's frames:**
Light that bleeds past its source. Grain that feels like paper texture. Subjects that are almost-resolved, never fully sharp.

---

### CAZSMIR

Color has been taken out of the world and given to one thing.

cazsmir's frames operate with a very specific logic: overall, the world is desaturated. The sky is pale gray-white. The concrete is pale gray. The air is pale. And then: one thing holds its color. The orange-red of a distant traffic light. The specific green-white of a fluorescent tube. The blue of a phone screen in a dark hand. One color, fully saturated, everything else drained.

This is not a grade applied uniformly. It is a decision about what the world has decided to keep. The world kept one color. The rest is gray.

His architectural framing is the other signature: the subject seen through something — a doorway, a window, a gap between buildings, the space between two columns. The architecture creates a second frame inside the frame. The subject exists in a frame-within-frame. This creates: the feeling that we are observing something that does not know it is being observed. We are looking through a gap. The gap is the entire point.

**What repeats in cazsmir's frames:**
A world drained of color except for one preserved source. The frame-within-frame. The observer's gap.

---

### AYXVFX

The image is being observed through something.

ayxvfx's frames have a quality of atmospheric interference — the feeling that there is substance between the camera and the subject. Not fog. Something more precise: a very slight chromatic aberration at the edges, a very slight grain increase at the borders, the suggestion that the air itself has density and that density affects the image.

His color is about contrast between specific opposites within the same frame. Not warm vs. cool globally — specific warm element against specific cool element, both pushed further than reality. A very specific hot amber next to a very specific cold blue. They do not blend. They exist in the same frame as opposites that heighten each other.

His frames feel like they were captured at a moment of transition — the second before something changes. This is not a narrative quality. It is a visual quality: something in the exposure, the motion blur, the slight instability of the image suggests this frame exists between two states.

**What repeats in ayxvfx's frames:**
The atmospheric interference layer. Specific color opposites pushed past reality. The transitional moment caught before it resolves.

---

### JABBAAAAAAA_

The frame is mostly darkness. The light fights.

80% of each frame is shadow or near-shadow. The remaining 20% is a single light source — and this source is allowed to be slightly overexposed. Slightly too bright. Not clipped to white, but sitting at the edge of what the image can hold. This creates: the light is fighting the dark. It is working. The darkness is present and heavy and the light is visible specifically because it is working against something.

His shadow color is the deepest of the six: blue-black, with a very slight purple undertone. Not as chromatic as Entergalactic's purple shadow, but not gray-black either. The darkness has direction — it goes toward blue and purple, not toward brown or green.

His frames are still. They do not suggest motion. They feel like they have been this way for a long time and will continue. The weight of the image is gravitational — everything tends toward the bottom. The light source is often at mid-frame or lower. The darkness collects above and around.

**What repeats in jabbaaaaaaa_'s frames:**
The high shadow ratio (80/20). The fighting light — slightly overexposed, working against dark. The blue-black chromatic shadow. The gravitational stillness.

## THE VISUAL DNA — WHAT ALL SIX SHARE

These are the properties that repeat across every reference. They are not aesthetic choices. They are structural rules of this emotional register.

---

**DNA 01: SHADOW IS THE SUBJECT**

In every reference, darkness occupies more of the frame than light. The ratio varies — anukitsu is softer, jabbaaaaaaa_ is more extreme — but in all six, shadow is not the absence of subject matter. Shadow is itself a subject. The dark areas are as composed as the lit areas.

---

**DNA 02: ONE SOURCE, ALWAYS**

Every frame has one dominant light source. A single window. A single streetlight. A single candle. The frame is organized around this one source. Everything else in the frame either is lit by it, or is in contrast to it, or is in the shadow it creates.

When a second source appears in these creators' work, one always dominates. There is always a hierarchy. The frame has a light and a shadow. Not two lights.

---

**DNA 03: THE SHADOW HAS COLOR**

None of these creators use neutral gray-black shadows. The shadows in every reference have a chromatic direction:

Kershaw: brown-black (slightly warm dark)
Entergalactic: purple-black (#1a0d2e)
anukitsu: blue-purple-dark
cazsmir: cold gray-blue
ayxvfx: deep cool blue
jabbaaaaaaa_: blue-black with purple undertone

The common direction: **the darkness in these frames travels toward blue and purple, not toward brown or warm gray.** This is how the eye reads the shadow as night rather than as indoor dark or shadow.

The AFTERIMAGE shadow color: **#0a1a1f** — teal-black. Not exactly any of the above, but in the same family. It travels toward blue-green rather than blue-purple. This is the one specific choice that distinguishes AFTERIMAGE's shadow from all six references.

---

**DNA 04: WARMTH IS EARNED**

None of these creators bathe their scenes in warmth. Warmth appears only where it is earned — where a specific, real source produces it. A practical lamp. A fire. Inhabited windows. A phone screen.

The warmth is always in contrast to a surrounding coolness. It is not the ambient temperature of the scene. It is a specific temperature in a specific place.

This is the warm-cool opposition that runs through all six: cold world, warm trace.

---

**DNA 05: THE GRAIN IS ALIVE**

All six have grain that does not behave like a filter. In each reference, the grain is heavier in shadows, lighter in highlights, and has slight color variation — it is not monochromatic. The grain moves differently in different parts of the frame.

This is the difference between real grain and applied grain. Real grain has behavior. Applied grain is uniform.

The goal for AFTERIMAGE: grain that has behavior. Heavier in shadows. Slightly color-shifting. Present but not uniform.

---

**DNA 06: THE FIGURE IS SMALL**

When a human figure appears in any of these six creators' frames, the figure is small relative to the environment. Not always extremely small — but never dominant. The environment is always at least as important as the person in it.

This creates: the person is inside something, not the subject of it. The environment has scale. The figure is evidence of human scale.

---

**DNA 07: SOMETHING IS WITHHELD**

Every frame withholds a detail you want. In Kershaw, the face is often in shadow or turned away. In Entergalactic, the interior of the warm windows is not visible. In anukitsu, the subject is soft enough to be almost anyone. In cazsmir, the subject is seen through a gap that limits the view. In ayxvfx, atmospheric interference obscures. In jabbaaaaaaa_, the darkness conceals more than the light reveals.

This is not restraint. This is precision. The withheld detail is the reason you keep looking.

---

## THE AFTERIMAGE IDENTITY — DEFINED THROUGH REFERENCE, NOT PROMPT

**The frame we are looking for is:**

A single warm element — approximately the size and warmth of a human hand or a window with light behind it — positioned in the lower third of a frame that is otherwise cold, dark, and large.

The shadow color is teal-black (#0a1a1f). Not blue-black. Not purple-black. Teal-black — the specific color of very deep water at night.

The warm element is amber (#c4854a to #e8a856 range). Not orange. Not yellow. Amber — the color of a lamp seen through glass.

The grain is visible and alive. You can see it, and it is not uniform.

The light source is practical. Something in the world produces it. You believe in it.

Something in the frame is withheld. The figure's face. The content of the window. What is behind the door. You want to know. The frame will not tell you.

**The test:**

Show the frame to someone who has never heard of AFTERIMAGE.
Ask: "Where do you think this came from?"
If they say anything involving the following words, the frame has failed: *AI, generated, aesthetic, filter, TikTok, Runway, content*.
If they pause and say: *"I don't know. A film? A photograph? Something real"* — the frame has passed.
